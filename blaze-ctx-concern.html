Concern Regarding Texas Blaze CTX Tryout Communication and Player Experience

OrganizationTexas Blaze CTX / Blaze Gibson
PlayerKassidy Cargill, 2015 player
ParentJeremiah Cargill

To the Texas Blaze CTX Organization,

Please forward this to Texas Blaze Fastpitch leadership, the National Director, and the appropriate CTX program leadership for review.

I am writing to respectfully raise a concern regarding the experience our family had with recent Blaze CTX / Blaze Gibson open practice and tryout communication involving my daughter, Kassidy Cargill, an 11-year-old 2015 player.

This is not simply a complaint about a roster decision. We understand that coaches have to make decisions based on roster needs, positional fit, team culture, and overall team construction. We also understand that not every player will be selected for every team.

Our concern is about the way the process felt handled, the lack of follow-up communication, the quality of the tryout experience, and the impact it had on a young player who showed up in good faith, competed, and was left with no closure.

Initial Blaze CTX communication

Texas Blaze CTX made a Facebook post looking for a utility/catcher with a strong bat. Within the hour, I responded directly to Coach Cozart regarding my daughter, Kassidy. I explained that she is a 2015 player with competitive experience, a strong bat, and experience at 3B, SS, catcher, and CF. I also shared her player page and hitting video.

The following day, before receiving a response, Kassidy happened to play against Blaze CTX 10U while picking up with Primetime 10U on May 16, 2026. Primetime won that game, and Kassidy went 2-for-3 at the plate, led the team in hits, and stole multiple bases. She was not simply a name on a message or an unknown player. She had just competed directly against Blaze CTX and performed well.

On May 17, we received a response stating that Blaze would love to have Kassidy come out to an open practice. However, that response also included an immediate emphasis on fit, effort, coachability, attitude, supportive families, competitiveness, and a team-first culture.

While those are reasonable standards for any competitive team, the timing and tone stood out to us. At that point, we had responded to a public post looking for the exact type of player Kassidy is, and she had just performed well against Blaze CTX the day before the response. Because of that, the added emphasis on family fit and culture felt unusually guarded and left us wondering whether outside assumptions or prior adult relationships may have already been influencing the process before Kassidy was evaluated solely on her own merit.

On May 31, we were asked to complete the open practice form so directions and field information could be sent.

Blaze Gibson tryout experience

We later communicated with the Blaze Gibson side as well. In those conversations, it was indicated that Coach Cozart would be assisting with selections and evaluations for that team. We attended the Blaze Gibson tryout in good faith.

I also want to mention the actual tryout experience itself. When we arrived, we noticed coaches with clipboards and each player wearing numbered bibs for identification. That part was understandable, but the overall structure and organization of the tryout felt below what we expected from a program associated with the Texas Blaze name.

From our perspective, the tryout felt poorly organized and more similar to a recreational-level tryout than the high-quality, competitive evaluation experience we expected. Each player appeared to receive approximately five swings at the plate, five pop flies with throws in, and five ground balls with throws. That is a very limited sample size to evaluate a player’s defensive range, arm strength, softball IQ, catching ability, bat quality, competitiveness, coachability, and overall fit.

The coaching staff also appeared to spend a significant amount of time looking down at clipboards, with very little direct player engagement. That stood out because several of the standards emphasized earlier — effort, coachability, attitude, competitiveness, family fit, and team-first culture — are difficult to evaluate without meaningful interaction, instruction, correction, encouragement, or observation beyond a few isolated reps.

In today’s softball environment, there are better ways to track evaluations without sacrificing player engagement. Tools like GameChanger, digital notes, shared evaluation sheets, video review, or post-tryout grading can support the process after the fact. But during the tryout itself, players need coaches who are present, engaged, communicating, and creating an environment where girls can actually show who they are as athletes and teammates.

Comparison against other team environments

This year, we intentionally made it a point to get Kassidy exposure to multiple teams and environments as part of her growth and learning process. She attended multiple open practices, tryouts, and playing opportunities, including Starfire, Illusions, Venom, Primetime, and Blaze. We saw a wide range of organization, communication, structure, and player engagement across those environments.

Based on that comparison, the Blaze tryout was honestly one of the more disappointing experiences. That surprised us because, fair or not, the Blaze name creates an expectation of a higher-level experience. We expected more structure, more reps, clearer communication, more player engagement, and a more meaningful evaluation process.

We also heard similar general feedback from several families we knew from Hotshots who attended or were familiar with the tryout experience. I am not speaking for them or naming anyone else, but I do think it is worth noting that our impression was not isolated.

Lack of follow-up and concern about outside influence

After the tryout, neither Kassidy nor Johnny Rogers received any follow-up communication.

That lack of communication is what raised additional concern for us. Both players showed up, competed, and represented themselves well. They were also the only two players selected during the Venom tryouts, where approximately 27 girls attended. That does not mean Blaze was required to select either player, but it does make the complete lack of follow-up feel unusual and discouraging.

There is also concern on our side that adult relationships, prior coaching associations, or outside opinions may have influenced how the girls were treated or evaluated. Kassidy has past ties through NCS to Brian Rogers from her earlier softball years, including 8U/10U Bananas and later Hotshots 10U, where he was involved as a coach. We are aware there may be prior tension or negative history involving adults in the softball community, and our concern is that an 11-year-old player may have been indirectly affected by things that had nothing to do with her effort, attitude, ability, performance, or fit.

To be clear, I am not stating that as a proven fact. I am saying that, from a parent’s perspective, the process created that impression and left a young player feeling snubbed, dismissed, and confused after she showed up and competed.

Requested review

At this age, how adults communicate with players and families matters. These girls are still developing, not just as athletes, but as young people. They remember whether they were encouraged, respected, ignored, or quietly written off. Even when a player is not selected, basic communication and closure go a long way.

We are asking Texas Blaze CTX to review how this situation was handled and ensure that players are evaluated on their own merit, not on outside adult dynamics, past associations, or softball politics. We are also asking that the organization reinforce a standard of respectful follow-up communication with families after tryouts, especially when a player was invited, attended, and was seriously considered.

We are also asking the organization to review the quality and structure of the tryout process itself. A limited number of reps, unclear organization, minimal player engagement, and little to no follow-up communication does not reflect the level of experience many families likely expect from a program carrying the Texas Blaze name.

Kassidy loves the game, works hard, and shows up ready to compete. She deserved to be treated as her own player, evaluated fairly, and given respectful closure after attending.

Thank you for taking the time to hear this concern. We respect the difficulty of running teams and making selections, but we also believe organizations have a responsibility to make sure young athletes are treated with fairness, respect, and care throughout the process.

Respectfully,

Jeremiah Cargill
